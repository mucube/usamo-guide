## Overview

Complementary counting flips the problem: instead of counting what you want,
count what you *don't* want and subtract from the total. This is especially
powerful for "at least one" constraints, where direct counting is messy but
counting zero occurrences is clean.

## Key Ideas

- **Complementary counting**: desired count = total − undesired count.
- Use it when the phrase "at least one" appears, or when the complement is
  simpler to count.
- Always verify: total − complement = direct count.
- Works well with probability too: $P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - P(\text{none})$.

## Core Skills

### Identifying When to Use It

If direct counting requires many cases but the complement is one simple
calculation, switch to complementary counting.

### "At Least One" Pattern

"How many 3-digit numbers have at least one 5?" Total 3-digit numbers = 900.
Numbers with no 5: $8 \times 9 \times 9 = 648$. Answer: $900 - 648 = 252$.

### Combining with Other Techniques

Complementary counting can be combined with casework: count the complement, then
subtract.

## Worked Example

How many subsets of $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ contain at least one even number?

Total subsets = $2^5 = 32$. Subsets with no even numbers (only from
$\{1, 3, 5\}$) = $2^3 = 8$. Answer: $32 - 8 = 24$.
